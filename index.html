Keep schools focused on learning

Schools should not allow ICE on campus without a judicial warrant.

Students cannot learn when they are afraid that school could become a site of immigration enforcement. Clear district policies protect safety, reduce fear, and keep the focus where it belongs: education.

Support a Warrant Policy See What We Are Asking For

This is not a call to ignore the law. It is a call for schools to require proper legal process before allowing immigration enforcement onto school property.

The basic principle

Schools are for learning, safety, and child well-being. Immigration enforcement should not be allowed onto school property unless officers present a valid judicial warrant.

Why this matters

Fear changes behavior fast. When students or parents believe a school may become a place of detention or family separation, attendance drops, trust erodes, and classrooms absorb the damage.

Attendance

Fear of immigration enforcement can lead students to miss school, avoid school events, or leave early.

Learning

Students under stress have a harder time concentrating, participating, and staying engaged in class.

Trust

Families are less likely to communicate with schools when they fear information could expose them to enforcement.

Stability

Even the threat of enforcement can create trauma for entire school communities, not just individual students.

A school cannot do its job when children are wondering whether showing up to class could put their family at risk.

What policy are we asking for?

We are asking school districts to adopt clear, written procedures requiring a judicial warrant before immigration enforcement enters school property or removes a student from school.

  • Require a judicial warrant before allowing immigration enforcement onto campus
  • Direct staff to immediately contact district leadership and legal counsel
  • Protect student records and personal information
  • Ensure students are not taken from school without proper legal process
  • Train staff so responses are consistent, calm, and lawful

What this policy does not do

It does not stop law enforcement from acting with valid legal authority. It does not tell schools to obstruct lawful orders. It simply requires schools to follow due process before turning a place of learning into a site of immigration enforcement.

Why schools deserve a higher threshold

Schools serve children. They already operate under special rules because students are minors, privacy matters, and uninterrupted access to education is a public good. Requiring a judicial warrant fits that logic.

Children are uniquely vulnerable

Students depend on adults at school to protect them during the day. That duty should include requiring proper legal process.

Education depends on trust

Families must believe that schools exist to educate and support students, not to facilitate immigration enforcement without safeguards.

Privacy already matters in schools

Schools routinely protect student information and follow special procedures because the school environment is not just another public space.

What happens when schools do not have clear protections?

  • Students stay home because they are afraid
  • Parents avoid school contact, conferences, and events
  • Rumors spread faster than facts, creating panic
  • Teachers must manage fear and trauma on top of instruction
  • Entire school communities lose a sense of safety

This is an education issue

The question is not only what immigration enforcement is allowed to do. The question is whether schools will maintain conditions where children can consistently attend, participate, and learn. A warrant policy is a school-stability policy.

What responsible districts can do

Districts do not need to improvise in a crisis. They can adopt practical procedures in advance so staff know exactly how to respond.

Adopt a written warrant rule

State clearly that immigration enforcement is not permitted on school grounds without a judicial warrant reviewed through district procedure.

Train staff before a crisis

Front office staff, principals, counselors, and teachers should know whom to call and what information they may or may not share.

Communicate with families

Publish the policy in plain language so families know their children’s school has safeguards in place.

NO ICE IN SCHOOLS — GUIDING PRINCIPLES
=======================================

Last updated: March 2026


WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS FOR
--------------------------
This document describes the goal of the No ICE in Schools website, the tone we aim for, and
how the site is organized to accomplish that goal. It is intended as a reference for content
decisions, discussions about what to change, and as a brief to give an LLM when rewriting or
creating pages. It covers what each page is trying to do and what it should say, without
specifying how pages are laid out or styled.


THE GOAL
--------
This site exists to advocate for one specific, achievable policy change: getting school
districts to adopt written policies requiring a judicial warrant before immigration
enforcement officers (ICE or other federal immigration authorities) can enter non-public
school areas or remove a student from school.

The ask is deliberately narrow. We are not a general immigration advocacy site. We do not
take positions on immigration law broadly, border policy, deportation policy, or the merits
of immigration enforcement in general. Our argument is about schools specifically, and it
is grounded in what schools are for: educating children.

The core argument is: schools can only do their job when students feel safe enough to attend
and learn. Immigration enforcement activity near and at schools demonstrably harms attendance,
academic outcomes, and family trust — and clear written district policies can reduce that
harm. Requiring a judicial warrant before enforcement access is granted is a reasonable,
legally sound step that does not obstruct law enforcement but does require proper legal
process. That is all we are asking for.

The secondary goal is to be a resource for the people doing the work: parents organizing
at their school board, educators who want guidance, students who want to know their rights,
and administrators who want to implement responsible policies.


THE AUDIENCE
------------
The site speaks to several different audiences, sometimes on the same page:

- Parents and families: They care about their children's safety and school experience. Many
  are from immigrant communities and may have direct personal stakes. They want to know what
  protections exist and what they can do.

- Students: They want to know their rights and understand what is happening to their
  community. They may be organizing or thinking about organizing.

- Educators and school staff: They want clear guidance on what to do if officers arrive.
  They also care about their students and want to support them. They are potential allies in
  advocacy.

- School board members and district administrators: They are the decision-makers. They
  respond to legal and operational arguments more than emotional ones. They need to know the
  policy is legally defensible, operationally feasible, and supported by the community.

- Community advocates and organizers: They want tools, research, and models they can bring
  to their local campaigns.

Not every page speaks to all of these audiences equally. The policy pages are aimed more at
administrators and advocates. The know-your-rights and learning pages are aimed at students
and families. The take-action and campaign pages are aimed at people who want to get involved.


THE TONE
--------
Professional and evidence-based. The site should sound like it was written by people who
have read the research, understand the legal framework, and believe in the rule of law —
including the requirement that law enforcement follow proper legal process.

Not inflammatory. We do not demonize ICE officers, attack the federal government, or use
language that sounds like protest rhetoric. That is not because we are neutral on whether
children should be afraid to go to school — we are not neutral on that — but because
inflammatory language undermines our credibility with the audiences we most need to reach:
school board members and administrators.

Not alarmist. We present the evidence honestly, including what it does and does not show.
We do not overstate risks or use fear as a persuasion tool. The research is compelling on
its own merits. Overstating it would give critics an easy target and would undermine trust
with careful readers.

Not preachy. We make arguments and present evidence. We trust readers to draw conclusions.
We do not lecture people about their moral obligations or shame them for not already having
these policies in place.

Empathetic but not sentimental. The effects on children are real and serious, and the site
should convey that clearly. But the emotional register should stay grounded — serious,
concerned, focused on the educational mission of schools — rather than mournful or
overwrought.

Clear and direct. Short sentences. Plain language. We avoid jargon. When legal or policy
terms are necessary (judicial warrant, FERPA, in loco parentis), we explain them.

Specific. Vague statements about keeping schools safe are easy to agree with and easy to
ignore. Specific, operational requests — adopt a written policy requiring a judicial warrant,
train staff using this protocol, communicate the policy to families in these languages —
are harder to defer. The site should always favor the specific over the general.


HOW THE SITE IS ORGANIZED
--------------------------
The site has four main content areas:

1. WHY IT MATTERS — The case for protective school policies, grounded in research on how
   fear and enforcement activity affect student attendance, learning, and well-being. This
   is the evidence base.

2. POLICY — What protective policies look like in practice: model language, explanations of
   why each element matters, and complementary policies beyond the core warrant requirement.
   This is the operational substance.

3. LEARNING — Resources for individuals: know-your-rights, student speech rights, links to
   external organizations. This is the personal toolkit.

4. NEWS / CAMPAIGNS — What is happening in the world: protests, policy changes, incidents,
   success stories. How to get involved locally. This is the action layer.

Supporting pages (About, FAQ, Sources, Contact, Volunteer) provide organizational context
and reference material.


PAGE-BY-PAGE CONTENT GUIDE
---------------------------

HOME PAGE (index.html)
Purpose: Orient every visitor to the site's core argument and call to action. Be compelling
enough that people want to read further.

Content: The page opens with the core claim: schools should not allow ICE on campus without
a judicial warrant. It explains why this matters in brief — fear harms attendance, learning,
trust, and well-being. It states what policy we are asking for. It explains what that policy
does not do (it does not block lawful warrants, it does not obstruct enforcement, it simply
requires proper legal process). It closes with a call to action — contact your district,
start a campaign, know your rights.

The home page should feel like a persuasive executive summary of the entire site. Someone
who only reads the home page should understand the issue, the ask, and why they should care.

Key image: A school hallway or school exterior — the visual should feel like an ordinary,
functional school, not a site of conflict or protest. The point is that this is what we're
trying to protect.


WHY IT MATTERS (pages/why-it-matters.html)
Purpose: Make the evidence-based case that immigration enforcement harms schools, with
enough specificity that a skeptical reader finds it credible.

Content: Opens with the framing that this is an education issue, not only an immigration
issue. Then presents the research:
- A 2025 Stanford study found a 22% increase in daily student absences after immigration
  raids in affected California school districts.
- More than 60% of high schools reported students missing school due to immigration-related
  fears.
- About 70% of principals report heightened student worry about family safety.
- Approximately 5 million children in the United States live with at least one undocumented
  family member.
- Research on districts with protective policies shows stronger academic outcomes and higher
  school engagement.

The page explains that the effects go beyond attendance — stress affects concentration,
participation, and academic engagement. It also explains that protective policies work:
districts that adopt them show better outcomes than comparable districts without them.

Closes with links to the threats and policy pages.


WHY SCHOOLS SHOULD BE SAFE (pages/why-schools-should-be-safe.html)
Purpose: Make the foundational philosophical and legal argument for why schools specifically
deserve stronger protections — going beyond the research to the underlying principles.

Content: Schools operate under a duty of care (the legal concept of in loco parentis — "in
the place of a parent"). That duty means schools are responsible for acting as a reasonably
careful parent would when it comes to student safety and well-being. Allowing enforcement
access without proper legal process does not align with that duty.

Constitutional protections that apply in schools: Fourth Amendment (unreasonable search and
seizure), Fifth Amendment (right to remain silent), due process, equal protection — including
the Plyler v. Doe (1982) ruling that all children have a right to public education regardless
of immigration status.

Educational psychology: children cannot learn when they do not feel safe. This is not
controversial — it is the basis of trauma-informed education, attachment theory, and decades
of research on learning conditions.

Closes with the argument that protective policies benefit all students by maintaining a
stable school climate, not just students from immigrant families.


THREATS TO SCHOOL SAFETY (pages/threats-to-school-safety.html)
Purpose: A practical guide for school administrators on specific, documented risks that
immigration enforcement activity creates for school operations.

Tone on this page: This page speaks directly to administrators. It is framed as preparation
and planning, not alarm. The opening explicitly notes that the goal is not to heighten fear
but to help schools prepare for plausible scenarios.

Content: Six categories of threat, each grounded in research:

1. Attendance disruptions — The 22% absence spike from the Stanford study. The UCLA survey
   finding that 63.8% of principals reported students missing school due to immigration-
   related fear. Framed operationally: this affects instruction, funding, and continuity.

2. Student stress and reduced learning readiness — 70.4% of public high schools affected
   by heightened student worry, per the UCLA survey. How stress affects classroom behavior,
   concentration, and academic engagement. The point that a physically calm campus can still
   be a psychologically strained one.

3. Bullying and school climate — 35.6% of high schools reported immigration-related
   bullying, per UCLA. The argument that school climate is not peripheral — it is part of
   what makes learning possible.

4. Rumors, misinformation, and loss of family trust — The first disruption is often not a
   direct incident but uncertainty. Families may keep children home based on incomplete
   information. Communication planning is part of safety planning.

5. Requests for access, records, or student contact — Rare, but high-stakes. Staff who
   improvise under pressure in unfamiliar situations make preventable mistakes. This is the
   operational argument for written protocols.

6. Enrollment decline — Stanford working paper: local ICE partnerships reduced Hispanic
   student enrollment by nearly 10% within two years. Effects concentrated among elementary
   students.

Closes with a practical summary of what schools can prepare for, and links to the policy
solutions page.

Sources section embedded on this page, citing the UCLA survey (with link), the Stanford
attendance study (with link), the Stanford enrollment study (with link), and the CGO
safe-zone policy research (with link).


POLICY SOLUTIONS (pages/policy-solutions.html)
Purpose: The comprehensive overview of what a full protective school policy looks like in
practice. This is the "implementation guide" page.

Content: Starts with the core policy: a written warrant requirement. Then walks through all
the elements a complete district policy should include:

- The warrant requirement itself: require a judicial warrant before enforcement access to
  non-public school areas; no administrative warrants or verbal requests; students cannot be
  removed without proper legal authority.

- Why the policy needs more than one sentence: a warrant requirement alone is not enough.
  It needs supporting systems to work.

- Response protocol: what staff should do when officers arrive. Specific steps: remain calm,
  ask for ID and documentation, do not grant access without documentation, immediately
  contact principal and district legal counsel, do not release student or records pending
  review, minimize disruption to students.

- Staff training: front office staff, school leaders, support staff each need different
  training. What training should cover: how to recognize a judicial warrant, who to call,
  what may and may not be shared, how to maintain a calm environment.

- Communication with families: why families need to know the policy exists. What that
  communication should include: published on the district website, summaries in multiple
  languages, shared at orientation and parent meetings, contact available for questions.

- Protecting student records: FERPA already provides protections; the policy reinforces
  them. No informal disclosure; requests routed through district procedure; only what is
  legally required.

- What the policy does not do: does not block lawful warrants, does not instruct staff to
  obstruct, does not ignore the law.

Closes with the argument that the goal is simply to ensure schools remain schools.


POLICY STATEMENT (pages/policy/policy-statement.html)
Purpose: Model policy language that a school board could actually adopt. This is the most
concrete, specific piece of content on the site.

Content: A full draft policy document that a district could adapt. Sections include:
Purpose, Judicial Warrant Requirement (with specific language about what counts as
acceptable documentation), Student Removal, Student Records and Information, Response
Protocol, What This Policy Does Not Do, Staff Training, and Family Communication.

The language should be precise enough to be legally useful but plain enough to be readable.
Each section should state the rule and the procedure, not just the principle. Note that
districts should work with their own legal counsel to adapt the language.


POLICY EXPLANATION (pages/policy/policy-explanation.html)
Purpose: Explain the rationale behind each element of the policy for people who want to
understand the "why" before advocating for the "what."

Content: Answers the question: why these specific elements? Why a judicial warrant and not
just any warrant? Why does the policy need to be written? Why does training matter
separately from the written rule? Why does communication with families matter?

Key arguments: The distinction between a judicial warrant (signed by a judge, based on
probable cause) and an administrative warrant (internal document, no judge, cannot compel
entry) is legally and practically significant. Written policies create consistency — without
them, staff improvise. Staff training matters because policy without training is decorative.
Family communication matters because a policy families don't know about cannot reassure them.

Addresses the counterargument that districts without recent incidents don't need a policy:
fear does not require a local incident. It responds to national news and community
uncertainty. A visible, published policy addresses that fear regardless of whether
enforcement has occurred locally.


ADDITIONAL POLICIES (pages/policy/additional-policies.html)
Purpose: Document the complementary policies that strong districts add beyond the core
warrant requirement.

Content: Four areas:

1. Sanctuary or safe school policies: go beyond a warrant requirement to a broader
   commitment not to voluntarily assist in civil immigration enforcement. Key elements:
   prohibition on district staff assisting in civil enforcement, prohibition on using
   district resources for enforcement, commitment to serve all students equally, regular
   reporting to the community.

2. Enrollment protections: Plyler v. Doe (1982) — all children have a right to public
   education regardless of immigration status. Practical implications: no SSN requirements
   at enrollment, no immigration status questions, training for enrollment staff on what
   can and cannot be required. McKinney-Vento Act protections for students experiencing
   homelessness, including those displaced by enforcement activity.

3. FERPA and student privacy: reinforce existing FERPA protections specifically for
   immigration enforcement requests. No disclosure of personally identifiable information
   without judicial warrant or court order. Designate a privacy officer. Document all
   requests. Do not maintain immigration status records in the student information system.

4. Language access: translation of key policies into languages spoken by significant
   portions of the school community. Interpretation services at meetings. Clear procedures
   for families to request interpretation. Regular review of language needs based on
   enrollment.

Also covers mental health and support services: what districts should do to support
students who are affected.


KNOW YOUR RIGHTS (pages/learning/know-your-rights.html)
Purpose: Practical guide for individuals — students, families, community members — on their
constitutional rights in encounters with immigration enforcement.

Tone on this page: This page speaks to people who may be frightened. The tone should be
calm, clear, and practical. It is not reassuring in a way that minimizes real risks, but
it is clear and actionable. The page closes with a legal disclaimer.

Content: Organized around scenarios:

Basic rights (apply to everyone regardless of immigration status):
- Right to remain silent. You do not have to answer questions about where you were born,
  your immigration status, or how you entered the country.
- Right to ask for a lawyer.
- Right to refuse consent to a search.
- Right to record or document encounters in public.

If stopped in public: Ask if you are free to leave. If yes, you may walk away. If no, you
still have the right to remain silent. Do not run or provide false information. Reference
to ILRC Red Cards (printable cards that state your rights).

If ICE comes to your home: You do not have to open the door. Ask to see the warrant through
a window or have it slid under the door. Check that it is signed by a judge and has the
correct name and address. Administrative warrants (I-200 or I-205) are not sufficient to
compel entry.

If someone is detained: Use the ICE detainee locator. Contact an immigration attorney or
legal organization immediately. Reference to Immigration Advocates Network directory.

Preparing your family: emergency contact plan, copies of important documents, attorney
contact information, childcare plan in case a parent is detained.

Resources for students and young people: United We Dream know-your-rights materials. NILC
guidance on school protections. Visual guides and videos (We Have Rights, Immigrant Defense
Project).

Finding legal help: Immigration Advocates Network directory, AILA attorney finder, Immi
legal screening tool.

Legal disclaimer: This is general information, not legal advice. Laws and enforcement
practices change. Consult an immigration attorney for your specific situation.

Key image: The page includes a photo of a young person holding a sign about students'
futures — conveying that students are active participants in this issue, not just subjects
of it.


SPEECH IN SCHOOLS (pages/learning/speech-in-schools.html)
Purpose: Explain student First Amendment rights so students and educators understand what
political expression at school is and is not protected.

Content: The foundational case is Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) — students do not shed their
constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate. Expression is protected unless it causes a
substantial disruption to the educational environment.

What is generally protected: political and social speech, peaceful protest and walkouts (with
reasonable time/place/manner restrictions), most off-campus speech (Mahanoy, 2021), passive
expression like symbols and armbands.

Important limits: substantial disruption standard, harassment and targeting of specific
students, vulgar or lewd speech (Bethel, 1986), school-sponsored activities (Hazelwood,
1988) where schools have more editorial authority.

Specific to this site's context: Students expressing views about immigration policy or school
safety policies are engaged in protected political speech. Schools cannot prohibit it simply
because it is controversial.

Key cases summary: Tinker (1969), Bethel (1986), Hazelwood (1988), Morse v. Frederick
(2007), Mahanoy (2021).

WHAT THE SITE DOES NOT DO
--------------------------
For clarity: the site does not take a position on whether immigration enforcement itself
is good or bad policy. It does not argue that ICE should be abolished or that immigration
law should be changed. It does not make claims about individual immigration cases. It does
not use the language of sanctuary cities or other broader immigration movements, even though
some of the policies it advocates for are sometimes called "sanctuary school" policies. The
reason for this restraint is not neutrality on whether children should be afraid to go to
school — we are not neutral on that — but because the broadest possible coalition for this
specific policy change includes people across the political spectrum who might part ways on
broader immigration questions but agree that children deserve a school environment focused
on education.

The site also does not make personal attacks on administrators or board members who have not
yet adopted protective policies. The assumption is that most of them have not thought
carefully about this issue and that presenting clear evidence and specific, operationally
sound requests is more likely to result in policy change than shame or pressure.


CONTENT PRINCIPLES
------------------
- Lead with the educational impact, not the immigration politics.
- Use specific statistics and cite them. Vague claims about harm are less persuasive than
  "a 22% increase in daily absences."
- When citing research, name the institution (Stanford, UCLA) and the year. Do not just say
  "studies show."
- Acknowledge what the policy does not do before someone else raises it as an objection.
  This is not defensive — it is honest and it builds credibility.
- Keep sentences short. Keep paragraphs short. This site is read by people who are also
  taking care of children, managing schools, and doing twelve other things.
- When in doubt, be more specific. A vague policy request is easier to ignore than a
  specific one.
- Avoid the word "sanctuary" in the core pages unless specifically describing sanctuary
  school policies in the additional-policies page. The word triggers partisan responses
  that distract from the specific, narrow ask.
- Do not use the word "undocumented" and "illegal" interchangeably. The preferred term
  is "undocumented." Do not use "illegal alien."
- The policy ask is about process (judicial warrant), not about politics. Frame it that way.

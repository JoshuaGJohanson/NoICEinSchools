The policy landscape

School policies on immigration enforcement have been tested, contested, and changed.

The landscape of school safety policies has shifted significantly in response to federal enforcement priorities, state legislation, and local community organizing. Understanding where policy stands — and where it is heading — helps advocates make effective arguments.

The legal framework

Plyler v. Doe (1982)

The Supreme Court established that all children have a constitutional right to public education regardless of immigration status. This foundational ruling has not been overturned and remains the governing standard.

Fourth Amendment protections

Non-public school areas have constitutional protections against unreasonable search and seizure. Immigration officers generally need a judicial warrant to access these spaces — the same standard that applies to other government actors.

FERPA protections

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act protects student records from unauthorized disclosure. These protections apply to immigration enforcement requests as they do to other information requests.

No federal mandate to cooperate

Schools are not generally required to assist in civil immigration enforcement. Federal courts have affirmed that state and local entities cannot be compelled to enforce federal civil immigration law.

The changing federal landscape

Federal enforcement priorities have changed

Federal immigration enforcement priorities have shifted significantly across administrations. During periods of heightened enforcement — including enforcement near schools and in communities with large immigrant populations — the pressure on school districts to clarify their policies has increased.

In 2025, as enforcement activity increased significantly, more school districts moved to adopt or update policies. The UCLA principal survey found that 77.6% of public high school principals had created plans to respond to federal agent visits.

Federal guidance on school-related enforcement has also changed. At various points, the Department of Homeland Security has issued or revised guidance on whether schools, hospitals, and other sensitive locations are protected from enforcement. The status of those protections is subject to change.

State and local policy developments

States with stronger protections

Several states have enacted legislation strengthening protections for students and school communities. California, Illinois, and New York have been among the most active in codifying protective policies at the state level, including:

  • State laws restricting local law enforcement cooperation with civil immigration enforcement
  • Guidance from state education agencies on school district obligations and options
  • State-funded legal resources for school districts seeking guidance
  • Requirements that districts communicate their policies to families

Pressure on protective policies

Protective school policies have faced political and legal pressure. In some states, legislators have proposed measures that would require school districts to cooperate with federal enforcement requests or prohibit sanctuary-type policies.

Courts have generally upheld the right of local governments to decline voluntary cooperation with civil immigration enforcement. But the political environment remains contested, and the durability of protective policies depends in part on continued community support and advocacy.
